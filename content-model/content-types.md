## Research consent form

## Who it’s for

Research participants who are:

- Professional users: public and private sector
- Citizen users: including those with accessibility needs

### User stories

> As a research participant, I need to understand my options, choices and legal rights. So I can make an informed decision to opt in or out

> As a research participant, I need to trust the user researchers so that I know I am taking part in legitimate research

### User journey

A user only gets the consent form once they've signed up through other comms and screening forms.

If, once screened, they’re recruited, they get the consent form

If, once screened, they’re removed from the study, they’ll get a link to sign up for future research. panels

## What it’s for

For people to understand the ‘legal’ rights and regulations of the research process

It's our record of consent for ethical and GDPR guidelines

It helps users to trust us, and know that they’re taking part in legitimate research.

It’s essentially part of the research session.

## Creating a new research consent form

This will happen when planning the research and a new project is created in Consent Kit. 

### Who’s responsible

The project’s user researcher will generate the templated text in Consent Kit.

There will be certain fields which will need project-specific details and the template text should be removed.

For changing the project-specific details, the user researcher should seek the support of: 

- a communications professional or content designer
- translator

Ideally, they would already be working on your project and understand the context.

The UR can write, pair write or trio write the project-specific text with these colleagues.

The translation, content or communications colleagues can provide editorial or peer review (2i) advice and translation support so that it is on-brand and matches our style guides.

### Workflow

To see a process that could help publish consent forms quickly and efficiently a [documented workflow for research consent forms on Miro](https://miro.com/app/board/uXjVKH9MO1A=/?moveToWidget=3458764611346414156&cot=10).

[https://miro.com/app/board/uXjVKH9MO1A=/?moveToWidget=3458764611346414156&cot=10](https://miro.com/app/board/uXjVKH9MO1A=/?moveToWidget=3458764611346414156&cot=10)

## Template and patterns

Each project generates a new pre-filled template. The majority is reused for each project.

### Template review schedule

We will review the templates and patterns every **[3 months/6 months/12 months] TBC.**

Gabi and Andrew own this process.

We’ll only change the templates if the following change:

- GDPR rules research process changes
- if users complain or give bad feedback
- alternative formats are needed

Reviewing the templates will be a UCD ops task between user research and content design. If we’re looking at the wider journey of a research participant, we may want to include a service or interaction designer to support.

## Measuring success

This is successful when:

- Research participants can access it and complete it
- It is usable and technically works
- We’re getting positive feedback from research participants

## Resource types

A resource can be tagged as one of:

- How-to guides
- Techniques
- ~~Tools?~~
- Templates
- ~~Case studies?~~
- (Research) reports (to add?)

Users can view a filtered list of resources, by their type, on the help and advice page.

When thinking about writing content, consider that:

- How-to guides can introduce techniques
- Techniques can introduce tools and templates

How-to guides and techniques can introduce links to case studies (blog posts) where this process has happened, or this technique has been used. 

## Tone and style

- Be friendly, but informative
- Avoid unnecessary narratives
- Write neutrally (not in the first person)
- Address the user as ‘you’

## How-to guides

How-to guides are informative pieces that might:

- describe a process
- offer step-by-step instructions
- show how to complete a task
- describe new ways of working

### Naming a guide

The title should reflect the content within and not make false promises.

For example, titles including

- ‘How to’: should explain how to do something
- ‘Benefits of’: should explain the benefits of doing something

You can write the name of a how-to guide as:

> Using the right technology
> 

> ‘How to test a product or a service’
> 

> ‘The benefit of testing products or services’
> 

The title must make sense by itself. You cannot assume that a user will also read the summary.

### Summary of a guide

Start with a statement of principle in the summary. For example:

> Making sure that Welsh content is more than just a translation helps to meet user needs and makes public services easy to use in either Welsh and English.
> 

You should always explain why the practice or way of working is important, the benefits or how it can help to meet the standards.

### What to include

How-to guides will often include a technique or techniques.

Create a specific resource for a technique, and link to them within a how-to guide.

These guides should be regularly reviewed.

## Techniques

Techniques are the methods to carry out a task or a process. They can be introduced within a broader how-to guide presenting a task or process.

We create techniques as individual pieces of content because they can be reused in different how-to guides. 

### Naming a technique

Techniques for a how-to guide called ‘Testing products or services’ could be:

Content crits

The name of a technique can be a noun or verb, depending of the name of a technique. For example:

- Usability testing
- Prototyping
- Content crits

### Summary of a technique

But the content’s summary should explain when and why to use it. For example:

> Usability testing helps you to understand how people use an existing product or service.
> 

### Contents of a technique

Each technique should describe:

- when it should be used
- why it works
- how to carry it out
- what tools you may need

Related tools can be listed on a technique page. 

## Tools

> ⚠️ I’m unsure if we’ll publish tools, or present them as links on how-to guides and techniques.

Tools are specific objects, apps or software that allow you to perform a technique or complete a task.

Tools may be presented as part of a technique. But they are also available in a filtered view of resources.

Quite often, tools will be links to other apps or websites. 

Provide a title and summary which gives the user enough context to know 

- why and when this tool is useful
- where the link will take them

## Templates

A template is similar to a tool, but it is something people can take away and reuse, such as:

- example job description
- user research interview questions
- consent form

### Formats

These can be:

- pages on the website
- links to online documents (google docs or SharePoint)
- links to templates on other websites
- downloadable documents (if no HTML version is possible)

### Title of a template

Make sure that the title makes it clear to the user what it is

It should not be the name of the file.

Summary of a template

## Case studies

> ⚠️ I’m not sure if we’ll publish case studies, or present them as links to blogs on a page

Case studies are usually `blog posts / or are they accessible end-of-project reports which detail achievement, challenged and how they did it and therefore separate from blogs`? They explain what people working on a project have done to change a service or the way a team works.

These can also be a gateway to link to techniques, tools and templates that a project may have used.

Don’t duplicate content

We don’t create two pieces of content about the same subject or technique. It’s better to improve the one we have published than add another.

If you are creating a new how-to guide, consider which existing techniques, tools and templates can be reused and linked to.

If you are creating new tools, templates or techniques, consider which how-to guides can be used to give users more context about them.
