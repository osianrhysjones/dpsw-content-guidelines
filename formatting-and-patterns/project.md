## Naming a project

When naming and describing your project, try to include a verb. This helps people to understand what the project is doing.

The name for a project on our website does not need to reflect the internal name - especially if the way we take about a project internally is not very descriptive.

Example:

> Improving content design in local government
> 

The internal version would have been ‘Local government content design discovery’.

## Project summary

The project summary is shown on the project listing page, but not on the page itself. It is a good place to mention:

- who we’re working with
- what difference the project will make, or what the project goal is

Example:

> We partnered with Perago and M-SParc science park to discover how the public sector can use digital technology to help Wales reach its net zero carbon emissions targets.
> 

## Project content

These are the suggested headings for each project:

### Project aims

Talk about what the project will achieve at a high level. This can be 1 sentence.

### Making a difference

Explain:

- the problem that needs to be solved
- why this matters
- what we’ll do to approach the problem

### Partners

List the project partners. You can also talk about each one’s roles and contributions.

### A summary of the work

This section can be edited regularly or can summarise progress. It can be about the:

- work we are planning, doing, or did
- the solution that we recommended or put in place
- the difference it’s making

Don't go into too much detail. Weeknotes and blog posts will do this for you, so you may not need this section.

Remember that blog posts (and weeknotes) can keep your project communicaitons fresh. 

> 🗣 Discovery projects will be more unclear. Project pages should be reviewed regularly and evolve during the lifecycle of the project.

## Extra content for completed projects

### Lessons learnt

This can include:

- what worked well
- what could have improved
- recommendations

### Next steps

Give a short summary of what will happen next. 

- Will the project be handed over to a team?
- Will it be taken forward?
- What difference will it make in the future?

## Blog posts

When writing blog posts on the CDPS website, these will be listed on the related project page.

This will keep the project page fresh and updated with your ongoing work.

Blog posts should bring out the lessons learned from your work.

## Weeknotes

Weeknotes and blog posts should show the progress of the project and give the user a clear overview of the project’s journey.

## Resources

We’re keen to start sharing all resources that the project uses so that others can follow our ways of working.

These could be existing guides, techniques, templates or tools on the CDPS website, such as discussion templates, research methods or Miro boards. 

If they don’t exist on the website, they can be added as resources to the CDPS website and linked to the project. Let the comms team know if you have resources to add to the project.
