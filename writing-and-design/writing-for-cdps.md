# Writing for CDPS

**On this page:**

## Be concise and create less content

Don’t duplicate content that already exists.

There’s a lot of content out there. If an authoritative source has already said what you’re planning to say, don’t clutter up the internet: link to the content that exists instead.

If we own the content, think about how you could add to it or improve it. Most of the time, creating new content about the same subject isn’t helpful.

Deleting content that doesn’t meet a user need is good practice as it means users will only find content that remains relevant to them. It also makes sure the website is more environmentally friendly as it does not serve irrelevant pages and information.

## Be open

Working in the open helps to:

- share your work
- held others to learn dn develop
- build trust
- attract talent

## Be relevant

### Meet user needs

Write only where there is a [user need](https://www.gov.wales/user-needs).

You should know:

- who needs this content
- what it will allow them to do
- which knowledge gaps it will fill

Try visualising the user(s) as a person and address your content to them.

### Reflect the words people use

Use the words people actually use when talking about your service. This helps people to find what they’re looking for.

Don’t expect you audience to use the same terms as you do internally. Use forums, social media and search tools, such as [Google Trends](https://trends.google.com/trends/?geo=GB), to find out the words that people use.

## Be clear

### Use clear language

In Wales that means adopting both:

- [plain English](https://www.plainenglish.co.uk/free-guides)
- [Cymraeg Clir](https://www.bangor.ac.uk/canolfan-bedwyr/cymraegclir)

Do not use formal or long words when easy or short ones will do. 

> ✅ Use:
> - buy
> - help
> - about

> ❌ Do not use:
> - purchase
> - assist
> - approximately

You can use [Hemingway Editor](http://www.hemingwayapp.com/) to help you to write clearly and to check for hard to read sentences in your content. 

Do not paste sensitive unpublished information into Hemingway, as it is a security risk. Use the [Flesch-Kincaid reading level tool](https://support.office.com/en-gb/article/Test-your-document-s-readability-85b4969e-e80a-4777-8dd3-f7fc3c8b3fd2#__toc342546555) (Microsoft) instead.

### Avoid j**argon and technical terms**

It’s not dumbing down, it’s opening up.

Avoid:

- unnecessary jargon
- legalistic prose
- unexplained abbreviations or acronyms
- rarely used Latin terms (for example inter alia, ad hoc, ibid)

We lose trust from our users if we write buzzwords and jargon. Often, these words are too general and vague and can lead to misinterpretation or empty, meaningless text. We need to be concrete, use plain English and be very clear about what we are doing.

Use technical terms where you need to. They’re not jargon. You just need to explain what they mean the first time you use them.

[Read more about writing for specialists](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/content-design/writing-for-gov-uk#writing-specialists).

### **Address the user**

Address the user as ‘you’ where possible. Content on the CDPS site often makes a direct appeal to get involved or act: ‘You can sign up for our community of practice by contacting…’ for example.

Say ‘You will get’ instead of ‘We’ll give you’.

### Use the a**ctive voice, not the passive voice**

Use the active rather than passive voice. The active voice emphasises the person or agent who performs an action. The passive voice emphasises the recipient of the action or sometimes the action itself.

**For example:**

Active: the dog chased the squirrel

Passive: the squirrel was chased by the dog

Avoid the passive voice because it:

- often uses more words
- can make it unclear who did something ('Mistakes were made' – by whom?)

A trick for telling whether a sentence is in the passive is to insert the phrase 'by zombies' after the verb to see if it makes sense.

**For example:**

'My homework was eaten... by zombies' is passive.

'Zombies ate my homework' is active (shorter and more direct)

When speaking to a user say:

You must attend both days

not:  Attendance is required on both days

## Be consistent

Keeping to a consistent style of writing makes sure everything we write:

- is accurate
- is easy to read and understand
- is punctuated consistently and correctly
- describes things in the same way
- uses the same style for things such as numbers and dates
- avoids common mistakes

## Be considerate

Think about whether the content is controversial or could result in media attention

If so, discuss this with the Communications and Engagement team.
