# Working with translators in projects and squads

Working with translators allows us to design services and products bilingually as part of a squad.

---

---

## Translators support projects, not squads

Translators will be allocated to projects that need support with bilingual research or design activities.

When a project is being scoped and shaped, SLT and seniors should consider these needs. However, this might not be clear until a squad plans the work.

We have more squads than we have of translators. Translators might be juggling multiple projects and translation requests, so it’s important to:

- plan bilingual design and research activities from the outset
- set shared expectations within the squad
- have certain flexibility to account for the demands on translators

> Delivery managers and product managers should lean on their researchers and designers to identify what support they need from translators. As we mature, translators and others might begin identifying those opportunities themselves.

## Translators and design

Depending on the context, translators might work collaboratively with designers to design:

- content
- interfaces and prototypes
- terminologies and taxonomies

Translators can help co-design and collaborate in activities such as:

- pair and trio writing
- pair and trio editing
- content critiques and reviews

## Translators and research

They might support researchers to:

- write research briefs and questions (when doing bilingual research)
- do simultaneous translation in research sessions
- take notes in bilingual research sessions

## Attending ceremonies

Translators are expected to attend:

- Squad or project-forming activities
- Project planning and alignment
- Sprint planning
- Sprint review/retros

The day-to-day involvement - such as the expectation to attend daily syncs - is up to the team to decide. This may vary by project and sprint - depending on the type of support a project needs and the amount of work involved.

## Translating project outputs

Translators supporting a project are not expected to be the default translators for written outputs from a project or a squad.

These include things like:

- team presentations
- research reports
- end-of-project reports
- website project page
- show and tell slides
- blog posts
- weeknotes

This is because these:

- often have a high word count
- need to be allocated between the translators at CDPS
- need to be part of the content publishing process
- need plenty of time to be translated

> The translator and/or Comms representative supporting your squad (or the #commssupport Slack channel) should be your first point of contact for these types of content.

## Translators in a team onion

Translators doing design and research might be considered either **core members** or **collaborators** depending on the project’s needs.

When a project or squad needs to translate project outputs, translators should be considered **collaborators.**

## Learning and improving

This is a new way of working for translators and most people in squads. We don’t know how effective it will be, so we want to keep learning.

If you feel it's not working in any way, the best route is to solve it within a squad: purposefully talk about this in sprint planning and retros, build relationships.

If issues persist, especially things related to unclear expectations, capacity or standards, bring this up with a line manager, project senior or profession lead.
